Withdrawal from Colonial Service and Formation of a Private Maritime Enterprise

By 1728–1729, Capuchino had begun to prioritize private economic initiative over imperial commission. After securing release from a further colonial assignment, he reorganized capital, liquidated remaining Paris holdings, and shifted his center of gravity toward Cádiz, where he established a permanent office and prepared vessels for sustained Atlantic operations.

The resulting Fránckentine-Capuchino Viceregal and Cuban Company combined coffee procurement, maritime transport, plantation management, and later selective intervention at sea within one increasingly centralized private structure.

Company and Fleet

Cádiz Office

A permanent administrative base near modern Avenida de Andalucía, symbolizing the shift from ministry service to private coordination.

Principal Vessel

Le Saint-Esprit de Saint Diodore de Cappadoce, the 32-gun galleon that became the best visual emblem of the company.

Atlantic System

Lima to Havana to Cádiz, with coffee, accounts, personnel, and later seized goods normalized within one commercial ledger.

Salvadore, Plantations, and Household Expansion

The maritime file is also inseparable from the Peruvian and Caribbean labor structure behind it. The archive gives major importance to Salvadore, whose familiarity with cultivation zones and local routes became central to Capuchino’s operations. The narrative also incorporates marriage to María Ávila Huancayo, plantation acquisition, and the birth of Cristoforo de Barclay Capuchino as signs that the company was becoming dynastic as well as commercial.

First Irregularity and First Seizure

Nassau Incident

The lowering of Spanish colors and refusal to submit to inspection represents the first unmistakable move toward operational ambiguity.

Nuestra Señora del Carmen

The encounter with the damaged Spanish merchant brig marks the first true coercive intervention: discourse, implied force, controlled transfer of goods, and the later normalization of acquired cargo within company books.

Operational Character

The archive repeatedly emphasizes that Capuchino was not a technical seaman. He delegated navigation and tactical command to experienced captains while he operated at the strategic and conceptual level. This distinction is important to preserve in the site: he is best understood less as a sailor than as a theorist, impresario, and commercial director at sea.
