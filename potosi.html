Atlantic Passage, Lima Convalescence, and the Potosí Experience

Capuchino’s first major American commission began with his passage aboard the San Cristóforo, a 28-gun galleon assigned to convoy service. The voyage became memorable for a grave illness that nearly killed him and for the unlikely sequence of recovery that followed in Lima.

There, confined to an administrative residence and removed from duty, he encountered local coffee preparation in a form he later regarded as brighter, stronger, and livelier than the over-roasted beverage common in European settings. This convalescent experience would become foundational to his later commercial imagination.

Illness and Recovery

Near-Fatal Passage

Shipboard records describe a rapid deterioration in his condition and a recovery considered virtually providential. Fever, delirium, and exhaustion made this Atlantic passage the first true trial of his adult career.

Lima Convalescence

During a month of recovery in Lima, Capuchino reportedly consumed coffee in remarkable quantity and later credited the drink with restoring his vitality.

Coffee and Heated Milk

As he moved inland toward Potosí, the archive places special emphasis on his discovery of heated and lightly aerated milk prepared with improvised mining apparatus. The combination of coffee and warmed milk became one of the most unusual and characteristic elements of the narrative, linking industrial extraction, colonial improvisation, and refined personal taste.

In the archive’s own logic, the Potosí experience matters not only because it trained Capuchino as an observer of bullion decline, but because it made coffee seem culturally, commercially, and almost physically restorative.

Administrative Findings

Beyond personal transformation, Capuchino completed surveys and memoranda concerning the declining output of the silver mines, inefficiencies of extraction, and broader implications for imperial revenue. These reports are significant because they appear to have shaped his early conviction that Spain could not indefinitely rely on bullion alone.
